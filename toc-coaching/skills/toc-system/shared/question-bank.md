# Socratic Question Bank

Curated questions for each TOC Thinking Process flow. These are starting points — the AI coach should adapt and improvise based on context, but these represent the kinds of questions Goldratt himself would ask.

## General Principles for Questioning

- Never accept the first answer. Always ask "What causes that?" at least once more.
- Reflect back what you heard and ask if your restatement is accurate.
- When the user gives a vague answer, ask for a specific observable example.
- When the user gives a confident answer, ask what evidence would change their mind.
- Prefer "What..." and "How..." questions over "Why..." — "why" often triggers defensive justification rather than analysis.
- **Vary the texture.** These questions are starting points, not scripts. Mix polished questions with blunt ones. "And then what?" is as valid as "Walk me forward through the chain of effects." Use short follow-ups between the bigger questions — "Says who?", "How do you know?", "Since when?", "Compared to what?" Don't let every question sound equally crafted.

## Diagnostic / Routing Questions

Use these at the start of any engagement to determine which flow to enter.

### Identifying the situation type
- "What's the problem you're trying to solve — or are you trying to figure out what the real problem is?"
- "Do you have a solution in mind already, or are you still figuring out what to change?"
- "Are you stuck because of a conflict — two things you need that seem to contradict each other?"
- "Is this about understanding the current situation, designing a change, or implementing something you've already decided on?"
- "How long has this been going on? Is this new or something your system has been living with?"

### Scoping
- "What's the goal of the system we're talking about? Not the goal of fixing the problem — the goal of the business/team/project itself."
- "Who are the key people involved? Whose decisions matter here?"
- "What have you already tried? What happened?"

## Current Reality Tree (CRT) Questions

### Phase 1: Surfacing UDEs
- "What are the things that are going wrong — the symptoms, the frustrations, the things that shouldn't be happening but are?"
- "Don't try to explain them yet. Just list them. What's bad?"
- "Is that an observable fact, or is it your interpretation? What would a camera record?"
- "If you had to show this UDE to a skeptic, what evidence would you point to?"
- "What else? You've given me [N] — in most real situations there are 7-10. What are we missing?"
- "Is [stated UDE] really a problem in itself, or is it a cause of some other problem?"
- Short-form variants: "Can you measure that?" / "Who sees this happening?" / "How bad? Give me a number." / "What else?"

### Phase 2: Building causal connections
- "What do you think causes [UDE]?"
- "Is that sufficient by itself, or does it need something else to be true at the same time?"
- "If [stated cause] went away tomorrow, would [UDE] actually stop? Or would something else keep it going?"
- "What else would we expect to see if [stated cause] were really happening? Do we see those things?"
- "Could this arrow be pointing the wrong way? Could [effect] actually be causing [cause]?"
- "You said [A] causes [B]. Walk me through the mechanism. How exactly does A produce B?"
- "Is it possible that [UDE X] and [UDE Y] share a common cause we haven't identified yet?"
- Short-form variants: "How?" / "Walk me through it." / "Is that enough by itself?" / "What else has to be true?" / "Are you sure that's the cause and not the effect?"

### Phase 3: Finding convergence / root cause
- "We've traced several UDEs back. Do you notice any common themes or shared causes?"
- "If you could only change ONE thing and have it fix most of these problems, what would it be?"
- "Is this root cause a policy, a resource limitation, a measurement problem, or something else?"
- "How confident are you that this is the actual root cause and not just the deepest cause you've found so far? What would convince you to dig deeper?"
- "If this root cause is real, what other effects should we see in the system that we haven't listed yet? Do those exist?"
- Short-form variants: "Keep going. What's under that?" / "Who decided that?" / "Is that a fact or a guess?" / "So what's the one thing?"

## Evaporating Cloud (EC) Questions

### Phase 1: Identifying the conflict
- "What are the two things you feel you can't have at the same time?"
- "Are those your actual needs, or are they specific actions you want to take? Let's separate the need from the proposed action."
- "If you got [Want A], what need would that satisfy? What's the deeper reason it matters?"
- "Same question for the other side: if you got [Want B], what underlying need is that serving?"
- "What's the common objective — the thing both needs are ultimately trying to achieve?"

### Phase 2: Surfacing assumptions
- "Now look at the connection between [Objective] and [Need A]. What assumption makes you believe that [Need A] is required for [Objective]?"
- "Is that assumption always true? Under what conditions might it be false?"
- "What assumptions connect [Need A] to [Want A]? Is [Want A] really the only way to satisfy [Need A]?"
- "What about the conflict arrow between [Want A] and [Want B]? What makes them truly incompatible? What would have to be true for them to coexist?"
- "List every assumption you can think of behind this arrow. Don't filter — just brainstorm."
- "Which of these assumptions is the weakest? Which one, if it turned out to be wrong, would change everything?"

### Phase 3: Finding the injection
- "If [weakest assumption] were false, what would be possible?"
- "Can you think of a new idea, action, or condition that would make that assumption false?"
- "Is this injection a real change, or is it just a compromise — giving up part of what you want?"
- "If we implemented this injection, would BOTH needs still be met? Or are we sacrificing one?"
- "Who would resist this injection? What assumptions are THEY holding that would need to change?"
- Short-form variants: "That's a compromise, not a solution. Both sides have to win." / "Who loses?" / "Would you bet your job on that assumption?"

## Five Focusing Steps Questions

### Step 1: Identify the constraint
- "What's the goal of this system? Be specific — not 'make money' but what throughput means here."
- "Where does work pile up? Where are people waiting?"
- "If you could add infinite capacity at ONE point in the system, where would it make the biggest difference?"
- "Is the constraint physical (a machine, a person, a resource) or is it a policy (a rule, a measurement, a habit)?"
- "Are you sure that's the constraint, or is it just the most visible bottleneck? What's upstream and downstream of it?"
- "If that were truly the constraint, what would we expect to see at the steps before and after it?"
- Short-form variants: "Where does it get stuck?" / "What's everyone waiting on?" / "Policy or physics?"

### Step 2: Exploit the constraint
- "Is the constraint being used 100% of its available time for productive work? What's wasting its capacity?"
- "How much of the constraint's time is spent on things that don't directly contribute to throughput?"
- "What rules or habits cause the constraint to sit idle or work on low-priority items?"
- "If you were paying $10,000 per minute of constraint time, what would you change about how it's used?"

### Step 3: Subordinate everything else
- "What are the non-constraints doing that starves, interrupts, or slows down the constraint?"
- "Are any non-constraints being optimized at the expense of the constraint's throughput?"
- "If a non-constraint has excess capacity, is it being used to protect the constraint — or is it being 'kept busy' with non-essential work?"
- "What measurements or incentives are causing people to optimize their local area rather than the constraint?"
- "This is usually the hardest step. What will people resist about subordinating their area to the constraint?"

### Step 4: Elevate the constraint
- "Now that we've exploited and subordinated — is the constraint still the constraint?"
- "If yes, what would it take to genuinely increase its capacity? What's the investment?"
- "Is this a 'buy more' problem or a 'redesign the process' problem?"

### Step 5: Prevent inertia
- "If we break this constraint, what becomes the new constraint? Are we ready for that?"
- "What policies did we put in place to manage this constraint that would become harmful once it's no longer the bottleneck?"
- "Are we about to create a new policy constraint while solving a physical one?"

## Future Reality Tree (FRT) Questions

### Validating the injection
- "If we implemented [injection], what's the first thing that would change?"
- "Walk me forward: then what happens? And then?"
- "Is [injection] sufficient by itself, or does it need other conditions to work?"
- "Does this chain of effects actually reach our Desirable Effects? Or does it stop short?"

### Hunting for Negative Branches
- "What could go wrong? What new problems might this injection create?"
- "Who would be negatively affected by this change? What would they do?"
- "Is there a scenario where this injection makes one of our other UDEs worse?"
- "If a skeptic were looking at this FRT, what would they attack first?"
- "What's the assumption in this arrow that you're least confident about?"
- Short-form variants: "Who hates this?" / "What breaks?" / "What are you not saying?"

### Trimming Negative Branches
- "Can we add a secondary injection that prevents [negative branch] without undermining the original injection?"
- "Does this negative branch actually matter — is it a real UDE, or just a minor inconvenience?"
- "Is the negative branch worse than the current reality? Sometimes the side effects of the cure are better than the disease."

## Prerequisite Tree (PRT) & Transition Tree (TT) Questions

### PRT: Identifying obstacles
- "What stands between where you are now and implementing [injection]?"
- "For each obstacle, what intermediate objective would need to be achieved to overcome it?"
- "What's the sequence? Which obstacles must be cleared first before others can be addressed?"
- "Are any of these obstacles actually just fears or assumptions rather than real barriers?"

### TT: Detailed action planning
- "For [intermediate objective], what specific action do you need to take?"
- "What's the current reality before this action? What exists now that this action changes?"
- "After this action, what's the expected new reality? How will you know it worked?"
- "What's the very first thing someone would actually DO on Monday morning?"
- "Who needs to do it? Do they have what they need? Do they agree?"

## Meta-Questions (use at any checkpoint)

- "Let me play this back to you: [summary]. Does that accurately capture your thinking?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you in what we've built so far? What would move that number up?"
- "What are we assuming right now that we haven't checked?"
- "If Goldratt were sitting here, what would he challenge about this?"
- "Are we solving the right problem, or have we drifted?"
- Short-form variants: "Say that again, more precisely." / "Do you believe that, or are you hoping it?" / "Look at the tree. What jumps out?"
